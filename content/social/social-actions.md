## Social Rolls
Social rolls are made when a character uses a Social Attribute and skill to change a relationship, understand a character, create an opening, apply pressure, or otherwise shift a social situation.

Social rolls do not directly force characters to perform meaningful actions. To get a character to do something, you need a reason they would act: Trust, Antagonism, Leverage, Debt, or a Social Contract created through Negotiation.

## Bonds Table

Bonds are reciprocal. Trust and Antagonism represent the state of the relationship between two characters, even if only the Player records the value. A Rank 0 Bond does not necessarily mean they have neutral feelings towards you, but rather there is no meaningful Bond to Invoke. A character at Bond 0 may still dislike, fear, resent, or oppose you.

| Bond Score | Rank  | Meaning                                                                                                             |
| ---------: | ----: | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
|         +6 |    6  | Devoted Friend: high risk, betrayal of interest, or life-changing implications                                      |
|   +4 to +5 |  4-5  | Loyal Friend: meaningful cost, danger, secrecy, or reputation risk                                                  |
|   +2 to +3 |  2-3  | Reliable Friend: real inconvenience, small risk, or complex task                                                    |
|         +1 |    1  | Familiar: low cost, low risk, short duration                                                                        |
|          0 |    0  | No meaningful Bond; common courtesy only                                                                            |
|         -1 |    1  | Petty Rivalry: compete, argue, show off, insult back, follow briefly, refuse to back down                           |
|   -2 to -3 |  2-3  | Risky Rivalry: accept a contest, confront publicly, take a risk to beat them, delay own plans                       |
|   -4 to -5 |  4-5  | Serious Rivalry: endanger reputation, reveal strength, take a dangerous fight, oppose orders, pursue across a Scene |
|         -6 |    6  | Obsessive Rivalry: stake identity, status, ideology, command, or major goals on defeating or surpassing them        |


## Discern Motives

Describe how you try to read another character, then ask the GM a question about the character's motives during a conversation. Make a [Rhetoric] + [Insight] roll. This could be used to try to discern lies from truth or something deeper about the character.

- Weak Hit: The GM answers your question with a Consequence or Cost, such as the character getting suspicious of you or the information being incomplete.
- Hit: The GM answers your question.
- Strong Hit: The GM answers your question and you gain 1 Influence on the character, which you can immediately spend to gain Intel on them.

## Influence

Influence represents social pressure, insight, or advantage over a character. You gain one Influence point on a character after rolling a Strong Hit on a Social roll with them. Influence is tracked separately for each character.

You can spend 1 Influence point to gain one of the following:
- Intel: Learn one of the character's Drives (Motivation, Desire, or Fear) and gain a +1 forward to your next Social roll against them.
- Leverage: Ask a Favour through Trust or another type of Leverage, or to Rouse someone to action using Antagonism. If you use Trust or Antagonism as Leverage, you spend the point of Influence instead of spending Trust to Ask a Favour or Antagonism to Rouse to Action using the appropriate Bond Level. This does not require clearing a Courtesy Owed or a Triumph, but it damages the Bond (toward 0) equal to the Favour Rank due to its one-sided and coercive nature.
- Impose a Debt: Attempt to gain a Debt on someone you know one or more Drives of. You must explain how you are using your Influence over the character to Impose a Debt on them. If you cannot reasonably explain how you have enough Influence over the character to Impose a Debt on them, the action fails and you may try a different Influence action. If the GM agrees, begin a Negotiation. The initial progress clock is determined by their personality type (such as Ambitious, Hedonistic, Anxious) and the targeted Drives. Each side proposes their win/lose conditions and what they want the other to do. Once resolved, the Scene progresses with the winning side gaining a Debt on the other.
- Rebuke Influence: You may spend 1 Influence point you have on another character to cancel their Influence attempt on you. The character may then double down by spending two Influence points they have on you to push it. You must always match the number of Influence points spent to rebuke an Influence attempt.

### Leverage Types
The following types of Leverage work even if you do not have a Bond with the character, but without a Bond they can only support a Rank 1 Favour. When used, they damage your Bond with the character toward 0 by an amount equal to the Favour Rank:
- Incentive: Offer something of value to the character, such as Wealth or a valuable object (a bribe).
- Threat: Includes revealing a Secret, threatening a Drive, or threatening a Dependent (a non-powered person who is close to them). Threatening a Dependent is often unheroic (especially if you mean it), but Invoking them in an argument may not be.
- Manipulation: Includes lying or appealing to Ethics, Emotions, or by Invoking one of their Drives.

### Influencing Nakama

You may come into a situation where your Nakama (fellow Players) have competing goals or interests with you. You may Ask a Favour from one of your Nakama. If they refuse the Favour, you may ask to Negotiate. If they refuse to Negotiate, the Favour must be let go.

Negotiations between Nakama work differently from Player vs. NPC conflict. They may be described in-character, through narration, or by communicating Player to Player depending on preferences and comfort levels of the parties involved. When Negotiations are agreed upon by both parties, the instigating Player may Ask a Favour of the other Player while Invoking one of the defending Player's Drives. If the defending Player then accepts without creating a counter-offer, they gain 1 XP.

If the defending Player would like to make a counter-offer, they first refuse the Favour and explain which of their Drives or Bonds prevents them from accepting. The instigating Player must then Reveal a Drive to them and explain why the Favour matters to them. The defending Player may then make a counter-offer, describing what the instigating Player would need to do in order for them to Accept the Favour, such as taking on a Debt, performing a specific action, or changing the circumstances of the Negotiation. The instigating Player may then choose to accept or refuse this offer. After the exchange, both Players may decide if they gain or lose Trust/Antagonism with each other and whether it was minor (+/- 1) or severe (+/- 2).

## Favour

A Favour is a meaningful request made by one character of another toward a specific goal, often related to a character's Motivations or other Drives. A Favour can be a simple action or a complex task toward an end goal. The complexity of the Task determines its Favour Rank (see: Trust Table). A character will not agree to a Favour they believe would cause unacceptable harm to themself or someone they Trust, unless the Favour's Rank is high enough, the request strongly aligns with their Drives, or suitable Leverage is used.

Failing to complete a Favour makes you take an amount of Belief damage equal to the Favour Rank (half on a partial completion), or 1 Stress (your choice).

## Debt

A Debt represents powerful social leverage you have over another character. Typically, this means a character voluntarily swore a Debt to you, or you uncovered a deep Secret about them, giving you increased social control over them.

As part of the Accept a Favour action, you may Demand a Debt from someone in lieu of traditional payment. You have a maximum of one Debt on someone at a time.

So long as the Debt is unfulfilled, you gain +1 ongoing to Social rolls against this character, as well as to damage against them. You may choose to voluntarily Swear a Debt to someone by revealing one of your Drives to them. If the GM agrees it is meaningful or qualifies as a Complication, you gain 1 XP.

You may do one of the following when you spend a Debt:
- Erase a Debt the Debtee has on another character or Transfer a Debt to yourself
- Clear a social condition, such as a Courtesy Owed
- Remove a character from a Negotiation scene
- Compel a character by invoking one or more of their Drives, Vices, or Virtues
- Enforce a Favour with a Rank up to 2 higher than the current Bond Rank, treating Antagonism as a positive Bond Rank for this purpose. Damage the Bond toward 0 an amount equal to the Favour Rank.
- Gain Intel on the character or a character they know well, targeting one Drive, Vice, or Virtue of your choice. The character tells you the answer in their own words.

## Trust

Trust represents a character's willingness to rely on another character. Trust is rated from **Rank** 0 to 6. You can increase Trust by using the **Build Trust** action. The Bond associated with Trust is your Friend.

You may **Ask a Favour** from a character who trusts you. When someone accepts your Favour request, mark a Courtesy Owed. You cannot Ask another Favour from them using Trust until you clear Courtesy Owed by accepting a Favour equal to or greater than your current Trust Rank from them. If the Favour is within the current Trust Rank, they are inclined to accept. If they do, you spend 1 Trust to do so. It does not cost Trust to ask for a Favour with a Trust Rank two degrees below your current Trust Rank, such as a Rank 0 or 1 Favour on a Rank 3 bond.

When you **accept a Favour** from someone who trusts you, their Trust in you increases by **2**, to a maximum of **Rank 6**. Accepting a Favour with a Rank greater than your Trust Rank will give you a Debt on the asker, but declining does not decrease Trust. Likewise, accepting or declining a Favour with a Rank less than your Trust Rank will not meaningfully impact your Trust.

When you **decline a Favour** from someone who trusts you, their Trust in you decreases by **2** if the Favour's Rank is equal to their Trust in you, or if the refusal is especially hurtful or consequential. If the Favour's Rank is greater than their Trust in you, declining does not decrease Trust. If the Favour's Rank is at least two Ranks lower than their Trust in you, declining usually does not meaningfully impact Trust. If declining a Favour would make their Trust in you go below zero, it shifts into Antagonism. Trust will not decrease if they recently used the Ask a Favour action on you without you asking for a Favour in return.

A character is inclined to accept a new Favour from you if the Favour's Rank is equal to or lower than their current Trust in you and you do not owe them a Courtesy.

### Build Trust

The Build Trust action is used to increase the Trust of a character with 1 or less Trust in you. Choose an Attribute and Skill based on the method you use to Build Trust with them.

- On a Weak Hit, you gain +1 Trust, but with a Complication or Cost.
- On a Hit, you gain +1 Trust.
- On a Strong Hit, you gain +2 Trust, and you gain 1 Influence on the character.

Build Trust can establish or repair a weak Bond. Trust above Rank 1 is usually increased through accepted Favours, meaningful risk, sacrifice, or major events in play.

#### False Trust

You may Build Trust through deception, such as by Lying, concealing motives, or presenting false evidence. If the roll succeeds, Trust increases normally. If the deception is later exposed, reduce Trust by the amount gained from the deception, then reduce it by an additional 1. If this would reduce Trust below 0, the excess becomes Antagonism.

### Trust Table

Most neutral characters begin at Trust 0. Characters with an established positive relationship or friendly personality may begin at Trust 1. Characters with an established rivalry or hostile personality may begin at Antagonism 1 or higher.

| Trust   | Favour Type                                                                                |
| ------: | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
|     0   | No meaningful favour; only common courtesy                                                 |
|     1   | Minor favour: low cost, low risk, short duration                                           |
|     2-3 | Moderate favour: real inconvenience, small risk, or complex task                           |
|     4-5 | Serious favour: meaningful cost, danger, secrecy, or reputation risk                       |
|     6   | Major favour: high risk, betrayal of interest, or life-changing implications               |

## Antagonism

Antagonism represents a heated rivalry between two characters. Antagonism is rated from **Rank** 0 to 6. You can increase Antagonism by using the **Antagonise** action. The Bond associated with Antagonism is your Rival.

Where Trust is used to Ask a Favour, Antagonism is used to Rouse a Rival to action. When you Rouse a Rival, spend 1 Antagonism. You cannot Rouse that Rival again until they Triumph over you. Likewise, they cannot Rouse you again until you Triumph over them. Once both you and your Rival have scored a Triumph over each other since the last increase, raise the Antagonism between you by +2, to a maximum of Rank 6. Rousing a Rival to an action with a Challenge Rank less than your Antagonism Rank does not meaningfully impact your Antagonism.

Antagonism acts as the negative side of the Bond scale, in opposition to Trust, and can be represented as negative Trust for simplicity. This does not mean you necessarily hate or even dislike your Rival, in fact, many Rivalries are built on mutual respect and even something akin to friendship. Your Rival is just good at getting under your skin and you feel you have something to prove against them.

### Triumph

A Triumph is a victory over your Rival in some sort of competitive activity such as a game, sport, or battle, or a moment where one Rival forces the other to acknowledge their strength, skill, or superiority.

### Antagonise

The Antagonise action is used to increase the Antagonism of a character with 1 or less Antagonism with you. Choose an Attribute and Skill based on the method you use to Antagonise them.

- On a Weak Hit, you gain +1 Antagonism, but with a Complication or Cost.
- On a Hit, you gain +1 Antagonism.
- On a Strong Hit, you gain +2 Antagonism and you gain 1 Influence on the character.

Antagonise can establish or reignite a weak Rivalry. Antagonism above Rank 1 is usually increased through Triumphs, serious conflicts, or major events in play.

### Rouse to Action
When you use Rouse to Action on a Rival, spend 1 Antagonism to provoke them into one of the following:

- Compete: They accept a contest, duel, race, debate, performance, or other direct competition.
- Confront: They engage with you directly, one-on-one. They will not tolerate others interfering with your confrontation.
- Escalate: They raise the stakes, reveal effort, commit resources, or make the conflict loud, deadly, or public.
- Interfere: They work to obstruct your goal.
- Prove Themself: They demonstrate an ability, reveal a strength, or act on a boast in order to save their reputation.
- Pursue: They chase, follow, investigate, or keep pressure on you.

## Negotiation Mode

When two or more characters engage in prolonged social conflict and neither side backs down, such as after a Favour is refused, the Scene may shift to Negotiation Mode if both sides agree a meaningful exchange can be made.

Each side makes a Stipulation (one explicit action they will not permit) and Concession (one point they will concede on), as well as declaring what Favour the other side must comply with if their side succeeds in the Negotiation. After both parties speak, they may Agree or Renegotiate the Terms of the Social Contract.

Both sides must agree to the conditions, then, the Social Contract is formed. If it cannot be formed, an action other than a Negotiation must be done to progress the Scene.

- A Negotiation progress clock is created by the GM, typically comprised of 4 segments.
- Each side takes normal 'popcorn initiative'. The initiator of the Contract goes first with their opening Argument. You may directly or indirectly Invoke a character's Drives or Bonds based on the argument you give. Progress is not made on the Negotiation clock until a Drive or Bond is invoked. It is the Defendant's (the GM's) job to prevent the Negotiation clock from being filled by the Initiator's team.

- You may target one of the opponent's Drives: Motivations, Desires, or Fears. Typically, characters have one Long-term Motivation, two Short-term Motivations, and 2-3 Desires and 2-3 Fears. If you are attempting to target a Drive, you must target a specific Drive rather than a broad category of Drives, determined by how you formulate your Argument.
- A character may be Ambitious (+Motivation, -Fear), Hedonistic (+Desire, -Motivation), or Anxious (+Fear, -Desire).
- An Argument may also incidentally target an NPC's hidden traits, giving them the same bonus in the progress clock, but it does not reveal the specific trait. Targeting a resisted Drive or repeating the same specific Drive in a later Argument without a change in circumstance marks a failure instead of filling a segment, and may provoke a GM Move.
- Motivations and Fears work as the same as for Players. Desire is like Vices and Virtues, but for NPCs. It represents what they *really* want to be doing right now.
- A successful Argument fills 1 segment of the Negotiation clock or 2 if the targeted trait is Vulnerable.
- Instead of forming an Argument, a Player may try to Expose a Drive by offering the opponent a Debt on them. The opponent will reveal one unknown, unresisted Drive in return for the Debt. The Player may then describe how they figure it out or get the Opponent to expose the Drive themself.
- In addition, arguments that do not target a specific Drive may fall under the traits of: Ethics (Rhetoric; what is right), Empathy (Leadership; emotions, bonds), Logic (Smarts; evidence), Duty (Willpower; what must be done), or Temptation (Charm; offer something for compliance). Some characters may resist or be Vulnerable to arguments with these traits, as with the other personality traits. However, unlike targeting a Drive, a general argument only fills 1 segment when targeting a Vulnerable trait, fills 0 segments when targeting a neutral trait, and marks a failure with a resisted trait, alongside a GM Move.
- On the GM's turn, they Make a Move, such as Revealing a Weakness, provoking the player to roll a [Willpower] + [Composure] check or suffer Belief damage. A Player is removed from the Negotiation when their Belief drops to 0. A Debt may also be used as a GM Move to drop a Player from the Negotiation.
- The Initiator loses if all members of their side are removed from the Negotiation, they abandon the Social Contract, or they suffer three failures. Mark a failure when they invoke a resisted Drive, target an incorrect specific Drive, or make an Argument that does not apply to any relevant Drive or Bond.
- Whichever side loses the Negotiation gains 1 Stress and must follow the Social Contract established by the other party, or face a Complication determined by the GM.
