## Disposition

Each non-player character has a Disposition towards other characters determined by a First Impressions check based off their default values assigned by the GM. A character's Disposition determines their reception towards different types of Influence Actions, such as Build Trust or Antagonize. Each Player may decide their own Disposition towards other Players or towards NPCs, but this value does not need to be tracked.

- Friendly: A friendly character places a baseline level of Trust in you. They expect you to do the right thing and will be disappointed if you don't. They will come to your aid, so long as it does not greatly inconvenience them, and are happy to hang out with you.
- Untrusting: An untrusting character does not necessarily dislike you, they would just rather keep you at a distance. They are willing to give you basic common courtesies or engage in small talk, but are unlikely to come to your aid unless they are Compelled to by their Drives, such as Ethics, Morality, or due to Debt.
- Antagonistic: An antagonistic character likes getting on your nerves and pressing your buttons. They may do actions that lead to you getting harmed, but would not directly harm you unless provoked.
- Hostile: A hostile character is one that is willing to directly harm you (such as roughing you up, capturing you) due to their Drives.

## Influence

You gain one Influence point on a target after rolling a Strong Hit on a Social check with them. Influence is tracked separately for each target. Unless a rule says otherwise, Influence on one character cannot be spent on another.

Influence Points can be spent on one of the following:
- Intel: Learn one of character's Drives (Motivation, Desire, or Fear) and gain a +1 forward to your next Social check against them.
- Leverage: Can use Trust (from Build Trust action), Rivalry (Antagonize), Incentives (Bribe with something they want or need), Threatening (such as to Reveal a Secret you know about them, or by Threatening their Drives. Threatening a Dependent -- someone close to them -- is often unheroic, but Invoking them in an Argument is not, such as "think of your kids") or Manipulation (Lie; appeal to Ethics, Emotions, etc.) to Ask for a Favour from the character. The type of Leverage used determines if a disposition shift occurs.
- Enforce Debt: Attempt to gain a Debt on someone. You must explain how you are using your chosen Leverage to Enforce a Debt on the character. Begin a Negotiation with a character you know one or more Motivations, Desires, or Fears of. The initial progress clock is determined by their personality type (such as Ambitious, Hedonistic, Anxious) and the leverage used. Each side proposes their win/lose conditions and what they want the other to do. Once resolved, the Scene progresses with the winning side gaining a Debt on the other.

## Favour

A Favour is a request for one side towards a specific goal set by the Favour. A Favour can be a simple action or a complex task towards an end goal. The complexity of the Task determines its Favour Level (see: Trust Table). A character will not agree to a Favour they know will cause harm to themself or someone they have zero or more Trust with. A character in a confused or inebriated state may agree to do something they otherwise would be opposed to, if the Favour appeals to one of their Drives.

Failing to complete a Favour makes you take an amount of Morale damage equal to the Favour Rank (half on a partial completion), or 1 Stress (your choice).

## Debt

As part of the Accept a Favour action, you may Demand a Debt from someone in lieu of traditional payment. You have a maximum of one Debt on someone at a time.

So long as the Debt is unfulfilled, you gain +1 ongoing to Social checks against this character, as well as to damage against them. You may choose to voluntarily Swear a Debt to someone by revealing one of your Drives to them. If the GM agrees it is meaningful or qualifies as a Complication, you gain 1 XP.

- Erase a Debt the Debtee has on another character or Transfer a Debt to yourself
- Clear a social condition, such as a Courtesy Owed
- Remove a character from a Negotiation scene
- Compel a character by invoking one or more of their Drives, Vices, or Virtues
- Enforce a Favour at the cost of Trust
- Gain Intel on the character or a character they know well, targeting one Drive, Vice, or Virtue of your choice. The character tells you the answer in their own words.

## Trust

Trust represents a character’s willingness to rely on another character. Trust is rated from **Rank** 0 to 4. You can increase Trust by using the **Build Trust** Influence action.

You may **Ask a Favour** from a character who trusts you. When someone accepts your Favour request, mark that you owe them Courtesy. You cannot Ask another Favour from them using Trust until you clear Courtesy Owed by accepting a Favour equal to or greater than your current Trust Level from them. If the Favour is within the current Trust Rank, they are inclined to accept. If they do, you spend 1 Trust to do so. It does not cost Trust to ask for a Favour with a Trust Rank two degrees below your current Trust Rank, such as a Rank 0 or 1 Favour on a Rank 3 bond.

When you **accept a Favour** from someone who trusts you, their Trust in you increases by **2**, to a maximum of **Rank 4**. Accepting a Favour with a Rank greater than your Trust Rank will give you a Debt on that character, but declining does not decrease Trust.

When you **decline a Favour** from someone who trusts you, their Trust in you decreases by **2**. If their Trust in you would go below zero, they may also take a Disposition shift. Trust will not decrease if they recently used the Ask a Favour action on you without you asking for a Favour in return.

A character will accept a new Favour from you if the Favour's Rank is equal to or lower than their current Trust in you and you do not owe them a Courtesy.

### Trust Table

A Friendly character starts with a Trust score of 1, while a Untrusting character has a base Trust score of 0. Antagonistic and Hostile characters may have negative Trust of -1 to -2.

| Trust | Favour Scope                                                                                |
| ----: | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
|     0 | No meaningful favour; only common courtesy                                                  |
|     1 | Minor favour: low cost, low risk, short duration                                            |
|     2 | Moderate favour: real inconvenience, small risk, or complex task                            |
|     3 | Serious favour: meaningful cost, danger, secrecy, or reputation risk                        |
|     4 | Major favour: high risk, betrayal of interest, or life-changing implications                |

## Negotiations 

A Negotiation begins when two or more characters engage in prolonged social conflict, such as after failing an Ask a Favour action.

Each side makes a Stipulation (one explicit action they will not permit) and Concession (one point they will concede on), as well as declaring what Favour the other side must comply with if their side succeeds in the Negotiation. After both parties speak, they may Agree or Renegotiate the Terms of the Social Contact.

Both sides must agree to the conditions, then, the Social Contract is formed. If it cannot be formed, an action other than a Negotiation must be done to progress the Scene.

- A Negotiation clock is created by the GM, typically comprised of 4 segments.
- Each side takes normal 'popcorn initiative'. The initiator of the Contract goes first with their opening Argument. You may directly or indirectly Invoke a character's Drives or Bonds based on the argument you give. Progress is not made on the Negotiation clock until a Drive or Bond is invoked, and Invoking a resisted Drive provokes a GM move and does not fill a segment. It is the Defendant's job to prevent the Negotiation clock from being filled by the Initiator's team.

- You may target one of the opponent's Drives: Motivations, Desires, or Fears. Typically, characters have one Long-term Motivation, two Short-term Motivations, and 2-3 Desires and 2-3 Fears.
- A character may be Ambitious (+Motivation, -Fear), Hedonistic (+Desire, -Motivation), or Anxious (+Fear, -Desire). They will also have a downside to targeting the downsides, such as a GM Move if they are a NPC.
- An Argument may also incidentally target an NPC's hidden traits, giving them the same bonus in the progress clock, but it does not reveal the specific trait. Targeting the same specific Drive (such as a specific Fear, instead of any Fear) results in a -1 hindrance instead of a success, possibly resulting in a GM Move, just like targeting a bad trait.
- Motivations and Fears work as the same as for Players. Desire is like Vices and Virtues, but for NPCs. It represents what they *really* want to be doing right now.
- In addition, arguments that do not target a specific Drive may fall under the traits of: Ethics (Rhetoric), Empathy (Leadership), Logic (Knowledge), or Duty (Willpower). Some characters may resist or be vulnerable to arguments with these traits, as with the other personality traits.
- On the GM turn, they make Make a Move, such as Revealing a Weakness, provoking the player to roll a Willpower Resist check or suffer Morale damage. A Player is removed from the Negotiation when their Morale drops to 0. A Debt may also be used as a GM Move to drop a Player from the Negotiation.
- Whichever side loses the Negotiation gains 1 Stress and must follow the Social Contract established by the other party, or face a Complication determined by the GM.
